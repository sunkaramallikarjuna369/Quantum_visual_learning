Superposition

Quantum Algorithms

Deutsch-Jozsa Algorithm

Given a black-box function f that is either balanced (returns 0 for half inputs, 1 for half) or constant (returns same value for all inputs), determine which in just ONE query using quantum parallelism.

Classical computers need 2^(n-1)+1 queries in worst case. Quantum computers need only 1!

Step-by-Step Tutorial

1

Understanding Qubits

Unlike classical bits that are either 0 or 1, qubits leverage quantum mechanics. A qubit is described by the state |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩, where α and β are complex numbers called probability amplitudes.

The magic: α and β can be any complex numbers, but they must satisfy |α|² + |β|² = 1. The squared magnitudes give the probabilities of measuring 0 or 1.

The Hadamard Gate - Creating Superposition

The Hadamard gate (H) is the key to creating superposition. When applied to |0⟩, it creates an equal superposition: H|0⟩ = (|0⟩ + |1⟩)/√2

This means when measured, there's a 50% chance of getting 0 and 50% chance of getting 1. Try applying H in the simulator!

Visualization of H|0⟩:

CNOT Gate - Creating Entanglement

The CNOT (Controlled-NOT) gate is essential for quantum entanglement. It has a control qubit and a target qubit. If the control is |1⟩, it flips the target; otherwise, it does nothing.

When applied to qubits in superposition, CNOT can create maximally entangled Bell states!

Bell State |Φ+⟩ = (|00⟩ + |11⟩)/√2:

These qubits are now entangled! Measuring one instantly determines the other.

4

Measurement - Collapse the Wavefunction

Measurement is where quantum becomes classical. Before measurement, the qubit exists in superposition. The act of measuring "collapses" the wavefunction to either |0⟩ or |1⟩.

The probability of each outcome is determined by |α|² and |β|² respectively.

Try the measurement demo above to see probabilistic outcomes!

5

Quantum Parallelism

One of quantum computing's superpowers: quantum parallelism. A register of n qubits can represent 2ⁿ states simultaneously after superposition.

This allows quantum computers to evaluate a function on all possible inputs at once - but extracting the information requires clever algorithms like Grover's or Quantum Fourier Transform.
